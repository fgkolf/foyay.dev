I recently came across the term `Laws of software engineering`. I found a list of 100 items and started reading them (there are several lists and the number of laws varies, with the most common appearing to be 13).

I've heard many times about best practices, principles, patterns, rules, and laws, but no one ever presented me with a full list of them.

After a few minutes, I was already converging on the religion these laws were serving. Any of the lists I found had common ground, and any developer would find many of them to be their compass when programming.

But wait... if these rules make so much sense, why aren't they treated like rules? Why aren't we given this list when signing our first SWE contract, or even at university?

And then I try to imagine reading them as if they were the actual LAWS a few years back. What would my understanding be now, and how would they affect my work?

TBH, I am not sure. I guess I would be less confused and chaotic at first. I would have faced fewer dilemmas, or at least reached a decision in a more deterministic way through a list of rules.

But there is also a bad side, and it lies in the fact that rules are meant to be broken. And breaking a rule should be an intentional move, well thought out and worthy of the risks.

But how do you build such a skill? I guess the best way is to actually break them: in your first years as a developer, probably due to ignorance, and later due to experience of things going wrong when breaking them in the past.

So not presenting them as a set of holy rules may be more of a good thing than a bad one. I guess that's why everything "depends" when it comes to programming decisions. It depends on whether it's time to break a rule or just follow it as usual.
